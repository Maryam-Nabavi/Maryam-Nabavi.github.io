The Watcher in the Woods

As a photographer, I'm drawn to scenes that reveal tension through stillness — and this frame captures that perfectly. What seems at first like a quiet forest portrait reveals, with a second glance, something more unsettling. The figure in the distance — barely visible among the trees — becomes the anchor of unease. They aren't centered, not spotlighted, just there. Watching. Waiting.

The interplay of shadow and light in this image is deliberate. It obscures as much as it reveals, forcing the viewer to search, to participate in the frame. That’s what I aim for — not just observation, but interaction. The stumps, the long shadows, the bare trunks — they all build a visual rhythm, but it’s the quiet presence in the fog that unsettles the harmony.

This image speaks to solitude, yes — but also to the feeling of being watched in places we assume we’re alone. Forests are ancient observers. They’ve seen things we’ll never know. This is one of those moments.

Current

Photography isn't always about the obvious. Sometimes, it’s about stepping in so close that reality dissolves into texture, tone, and movement. This piece captures that dissolution.

"Current" is both literal and emotional. The motion resembles a wave, a tide, a breath—something between water and wind, but grounded in neither. I wanted the viewer to feel like they’re peering into something alive, yet unfamiliar. The softness of the lines contrasts the depth of the shadows, creating a pull—subtle, but undeniable.

This image asks for pause. It doesn’t reveal its subject at first glance, and that’s intentional. Photography, at its most powerful, creates questions. What is this? Why does it feel so tactile? What memory does it echo? This is not about documentation—it’s about evocation.

Undercurrent

As a photographer, I often find that the most powerful compositions aren’t about the subject itself — but about what the subject suggests. In this frame, the water’s movement becomes more than motion; it becomes language.

What drew me to this moment wasn’t just the texture of the ripples or the tonal depth — it was the subtle tension beneath the calm. The light barely touches the wave crests, suggesting a fragile balance between chaos and stillness. Every soft line is a memory in motion, and every shadow hints at something deeper, something unspoken.

“Undercurrent” isn’t meant to explain. It’s meant to resonate — to stir the viewer in the same way wind stirs water: invisibly, but undeniably.
